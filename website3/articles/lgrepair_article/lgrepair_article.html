For Appliance Buyers, the Journey Breaks Down When the Repair Process Begins

LG’s weakest post-purchase moment is not product setup or delivery, but the point when owners need technical help and discover how hard service is to secure.

Vitor Perrone May 19 2026

Danielle had owned her refrigerator for just over a month when the ice maker stopped working. She called customer service and spent 15 minutes running through diagnostics with an agent who assured her a technician would contact her within 48 hours. No one did. When she phoned again, she was told the first representative "must have forgot" to assign the repair, forcing her to repeat the same 15-minute script from the beginning. The appliance was still in her kitchen, still under warranty, and suddenly already in the hands of a support system that seemed unable to move a basic service request forward.

Her experience fits a pattern that cuts across brands. One Samsung buyer said a French Door refrigerator installed in December failed by the end of January; a technician identified a freon leak, the replacement part was shipped to the home, and then the service ticket was closed before anyone returned, leaving the family nearly three weeks without a working refrigerator. A GE owner described a two-year-old unit that stopped cooling, followed by technicians who missed service windows by four hours, arrived without the needed part, and then never came back because the component was on back order, costing the customer three missed workdays and a $120 reimbursement that still had not appeared. Another reviewer recounted buying a refrigerator in August 2024, losing all freon by February 2025, and still being stuck without a working unit after repeated calls, four different service-company attempts, ordered parts, and a no-show appointment at the end of March.

That is the backdrop for LG’s weakest moment in the appliance journey in 2025 Q4. When consumers try to seek troubleshooting support online or by phone after purchase, the brand scores just 0.52 out of 10 in Decomposer.ai’s review-based analysis. The industry average is already low at 3.01, but LG falls far below even that modest bar. More important, this is not a fringe complaint: 62% of consumers mention this activity, making technical support not only the worst relevant score in the journey, but one of the most widely felt pain points in the category.

That matters because appliance brands do not lose trust only when a refrigerator fails; they lose it when the recovery process turns into a second failure. A broken compressor, leaking freon line, or faulty ice maker can often be explained away as a product issue. What customers remember is the stalled ticket, the missed appointment, the part that exists but never arrives with the technician, and the workday lost waiting at home. For manufacturers, the service chain is now part of the product. Warranty promises, authorized repair networks, call-center scripts, and parts logistics shape the ownership experience as much as stainless steel finishes or energy-efficiency claims.

The contradiction is hard to miss. Brands have more ways than ever to track cases, automate scheduling, route parts, and flag repeat-contact failures before they spiral. Yet owners still find themselves retelling the same story to different agents while food spoils in silent kitchens. The tools exist, the complaints are widespread, and the breakdown is plainly visible. What remains unclear is why the most predictable moment of need is still where the appliance journey collapses.
