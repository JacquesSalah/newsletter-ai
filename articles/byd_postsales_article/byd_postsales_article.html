BYD’s biggest breakdown comes after the sale, when routine service turns into a dead end

For BYD owners in late 2025, the dealership visit meant to keep a new car on the road is emerging as the weakest point in the brand’s customer journey.

Davi Zimbardi May 19 2026

Seven months after buying a new BYD Song Plus GS DM 2024, with just 8,350 kilometers on the odometer, Juliana was driving with her daughter when the side and curtain airbags deployed by themselves, without any collision. The car lost power and stopped at the exit of a bridge, turning a family trip into a roadside emergency. At BYD Saga in Cuiabá, she says, the aftermath was almost as alarming as the incident itself: the dealership denied warranty coverage and presented an estimate of more than R$72,000, backed by what the family describes as an incoherent technical report.

The pattern does not end there. In Manaus, a driver who bought a BYD Dolphin Mini in June says he took the car to BYD Parvi on October 16 after warning lights appeared and the vehicle began losing power; he was told it was ready, only to find the defect unchanged, and says the car has remained at the dealership since the next day with no repair deadline while he loses income as an app driver. In Nova Iguaçu, a customer reports that his vehicle has been stuck at BYD Itavema since October 21 with recurring sound system and alarm failures that had already surfaced three times before, while a promised loaner had still not been released by October 24 and communication had become, in his words, impossible. In Novo Hamburgo, another owner says a car has been sitting since August 11 at an insurance-authorized repair shop because IESA Nissan still had not received replacement parts after 74 days, with delivery postponed for the third time.

Those stories help explain why BYD’s steepest collapse in the customer journey is not at the moment of purchase, but after it. In Decomposer.ai’s 2025 Q4 reading, car servicing at the dealership in the post-purchase phase scored just 1.72 out of 10 for BYD, well below the industry average of 2.6. More striking is the scale: 61.7% of consumers mention this activity, making it not only poorly rated but broadly experienced. A weak score in a niche touchpoint is a problem; a weak score in one of the most recurring touchpoints is a structural fault line.

That matters because the after-sales visit is where automakers either convert first-time buyers into repeat customers or start eroding trust while the car is still new. For a fast-growing brand such as BYD, dealership service is not a back-office function; it is the proof point that the brand can support the volume it has already sold. Delays, unclear diagnostics, parts shortages and silent dealerships hit harder in electric and hybrid adoption, where many owners are still building their confidence in unfamiliar technology and have less tolerance for uncertainty when something goes wrong.

The contradiction is hard to ignore. BYD is selling the promise of a new automotive future, yet for many owners the decisive moment is a routine return to the dealership that feels opaque, slow and unresolved. The systems that should prevent this already exist—diagnostic protocols, parts planning, loaner logistics, customer communication—but the complaints suggest they are not reaching the service lane consistently. The question now is not whether after-sales has become a bottleneck, but how long a brand can keep growing while one of its most common customer interactions remains its worst one.
